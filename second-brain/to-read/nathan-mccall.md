## 1. Synthetic Overlapping Genes Stabilize Genetic Systems (2026)

**Journal:** mBio  
**Authors:** SP Leonard, TM Halvorsen, B Lim, NA McCall, DM Park, Y Jiao, MC Yung, et al.  
**Link:** https://journals.asm.org/doi/abs/10.1128/mbio.02725-25

**Abstract:**
Overlapping genes — wherein two different proteins are translated from alternative reading frames of the same DNA sequence — provide a means to stabilize an engineered gene by directly linking its evolutionary fate with that of an overlapping gene. However, creating overlapping gene pairs is challenging, as it requires redesigning both protein products to accommodate overlap constraints. This paper presents a new "overlapping, alternate-frame insertion" (OAFI) method for creating synthetic overlapping genes by inserting an "inner" gene, encoded in an alternate frame, into a flexible region of an "outer" gene. Using OAFI, new overlapping gene pairs of genetic reporters and bacterial toxins are created within an antibiotic resistance gene. Both inner and outer genes retain function despite redesign, with translation of the inner gene influenced by its overlap position in the outer gene.

## 2. Iron Limitation Alters Diatom Carbon Flow Through Shifts in Microbiome Exometabolite Consumption (2026)

**Journal:** bioRxiv (preprint)  
**Authors:** Naomi E Gilbert, Nicole R Coffey, Nathan A McCall, Jeff Kimbrel, Craig A Carlson, Elisa Halewood, Christina E Ramon, Patrick Diep, Peter K Weber, Xavier Mayali, Rene M Boiteau, Rhona K Stuart  
**Link:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2026.03.16.711593

**Abstract:**
Iron is required for photosynthesis, and thus affects biogeochemical cycling in widespread regions where its availability is limited. Turnover of aquatic photosynthetically-derived carbon is largely constrained by bacterial activity, but we lack a mechanistic understanding of how iron limitation influences this activity. This study examines a bacterial enrichment community dependent on carbon from the diatom *Phaeodactylum tricornutum* to investigate how iron limitation alters carbon flow to bacteria, and exometabolite and community composition. Using stable isotope tracing, diatom exudate incorporation was quantified with single-cell-resolution. A population of bacteria under iron limitation showed high metabolic activity yet low incorporation of newly-fixed diatom carbon, indicating a metabolism shift relative to the iron-replete control. Ultra-high-resolution exometabolomics revealed bacterial consumption of aromatics, lipid-like compounds, and purines/pyrimidines under iron-limitation. Results provide a mechanistic link between iron-limitation driven shifts in exudate composition and flow of carbon to the microbiome — with implications for predicting carbon flow in surface oceans and engineering algal-bacterial systems.

## 3. Spatially Structured Bacterial Interactions Alter Algal Carbon Flow to Bacteria (2025)

**Journal:** The ISME Journal, Vol. 19(1), wraf096  
**Authors:** Hyungseok Kim, Vanessa L Brisson, John R Casey, Courtney Swink, Kristina A Rolison, Nathan McCall, Amber N Golini, Trent R Northen, Dušan Veličković, Peter K Weber, Cullen R Buie, Xavier Mayali, Rhona K Stuart  
**Link:** https://academic.oup.com/ismej/article/19/1/wraf096/7706200

**Abstract:**
Phytoplankton account for nearly half of global photosynthetic carbon fixation, and the fate of that carbon is regulated in large part by microbial food web processing. This study investigates how interactions among heterotrophic bacteria impact the fate of photosynthetically fixed carbon using bacterial isolates capable of growing on exudates from the diatom *Phaeodactylum tricornutum*. With exometabolomics and genome-scale metabolic modeling, the degree of resource competition between bacterial pairs was estimated. In sequential spent media experiments, pairwise interactions were found to be more beneficial than predicted based on resource competition alone, with 30% exhibiting facilitative interactions — providing insight into how bacteria-bacteria interactions shape carbon remineralization and biomass incorporation.

## 4. Accessible Type 2 Diabetes Medication Through Stable Expression of Exendin-4 in *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (2024)

**Journal:** Frontiers in Systems Biology, Vol. 4, Article 1283371  
**Authors:** Gia Balius, Kiana Imani, Zoë Petroff, Elizabeth Beer, Thiago Brasileiro Feitosa, Nathan McCall, Lauren Paule, Neo Yixuan Peng, Joanne Shen, Vidhata Singh, Cambell Strand, Jonathan Zau, DL Bernick  
**Link:** https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fsysb.2024.1283371

**Abstract:**
Diabetes mellitus affects roughly one in ten people globally and is the world's ninth leading cause of death; however, chronic complications contributing to mortality can largely be prevented with proper treatment. Glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonists, such as Exendin-4, are among the leading Type 2 diabetes treatments but are prohibitively expensive. This study designed experimental models for recombinant Exendin-4 protein production in both *Escherichia coli* and *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*. Protein expression in the chromosomally integrated *S. cerevisiae* strain was observed at the expected size and confirmed by immunoassay — providing a foundation for using this Generally Regarded as Safe organism as an affordable, locally-distributable diabetes treatment.
